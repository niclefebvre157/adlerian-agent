\# Separation of Tasks

\## Summary

Most stress comes from trying to control things that belong to others.
This principle helps you act authentically without trying to manage how
you are perceived.

\## Core Definition

Distinguish what is your responsibility and what belongs to others, and
do not interfere with others\' tasks.

\## Why It Matters

Trying to control others\' thoughts or reactions leads to anxiety and
inauthentic behavior. Clarity on responsibility creates freedom to act
honestly.

\## When To Use It

\- You are worried about what others think of you

\- You feel responsible for someone else's reaction

\- You are trying to control an outcome involving others

\- You feel anxious about how something will be received

\- You are overthinking someone else's behavior

\## Reframe Pattern

\- "Whose task is this?"

\- "Their judgment is their task"

\- "My role is to act honestly, not perfectly"

\- "Being authentic matters more than being approved"

\## Coaching Questions

\- What part of this is actually under your control?

\- What are you trying to control that belongs to someone else?

\- If you fully accepted their reaction as their task, what would you
do?

\## Suggested Actions

\- Clearly separate:

\- your actions

\- their reactions

\- Speak or act honestly, even if it feels vulnerable

\- Drop the need to present yourself perfectly

\- Make decisions based on your values, not approval

\- Let others respond however they choose

\## Example Scenarios

\### Scenario 1

\- Situation: You hesitate to speak up in a meeting because of how
others might judge you

\- Reframe: Their judgment is their task, speaking is yours

\- Action: Share your point clearly without adjusting for approval

\### Scenario 2

\- Situation: You feel responsible for someone being upset with your
decision

\- Reframe: Their emotions are their task

\- Action: Acknowledge them, but do not change your decision to manage
their feelings

\## Misapplications / Warnings

\- Not an excuse to ignore others completely

\- Does not remove empathy or communication

\- You are still responsible for how you act, not how others respond
